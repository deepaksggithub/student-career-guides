Your Path to Law School

A comprehensive guide for high school students who dream of becoming lawyers. From the courses you should take today to the volunteer work that sets you apart.

Start Your Journey

The Roadmap to Law School

Becoming a lawyer is a long but rewarding journey. Here's an overview of the key milestones from high school through passing the bar exam.

High School (Grades 9–12)

Build a strong academic foundation. Take challenging courses in English, history, government, and debate. Join Mock Trial or speech & debate clubs. Maintain a high GPA.

Undergraduate Degree (4 Years)

Earn a bachelor's degree in any field. There is no required "pre-law" major, but Political Science, English, History, Philosophy, and Economics are popular choices that build relevant skills.

LSAT Exam

Take the Law School Admission Test, typically during junior or senior year of college. This standardized test is a major factor in law school admissions. Aim for the highest score possible.

Law School (3 Years)

Attend an ABA-accredited law school to earn your Juris Doctor (J.D.) degree. You'll study constitutional law, contracts, torts, civil procedure, criminal law, and more.

Bar Exam & Licensure

After law school, pass the bar exam in the state where you plan to practice. Once licensed, you're officially an attorney and can begin your legal career.

High School Activities

Beyond coursework, these activities demonstrate your passion for law and build real skills.

Competition

Mock Trial

Simulate courtroom proceedings by playing the roles of attorneys and witnesses. This is the single best high school activity for aspiring lawyers.

  • Learn courtroom procedures and legal terminology
  • Practice constructing and presenting legal arguments
  • Develop quick thinking under cross-examination
  • Network with attorney coaches and judges
Competition

Speech & Debate

Formal debate teaches the skills of persuasion, evidence analysis, and structured argumentation that are central to legal practice.

  • Lincoln-Douglas debate focuses on values and ethics
  • Policy debate builds research and fast thinking skills
  • Public Forum debate develops accessible communication
  • Congressional debate simulates legislative process
Leadership

Student Government

Participating in student government develops leadership, negotiation, and an understanding of governance and policy-making.

  • Draft and propose school policies
  • Practice consensus-building and compromise
  • Develop public speaking and leadership skills
  • Manage budgets and organize events

Volunteering & Experience

Law school admissions committees value demonstrated commitment to justice and community service. Volunteering shows that you understand the real-world impact of the law.

⚖

Legal Aid Organizations

Volunteer with local Legal Aid societies that provide free legal services to low-income individuals. Tasks may include intake interviews, filing paperwork, and assisting attorneys.

Where to find: Search for your local Legal Aid Society, Legal Services Corporation affiliates, or law school clinical programs that accept volunteers.
🏫

Court Observation & Clerking

Many local courthouses allow students to observe proceedings. Some courts and judges' chambers offer internship or volunteer opportunities for students.

Where to find: Contact your county or municipal courthouse. Ask about courtroom observer programs or volunteer positions in the clerk's office.
👥

Civil Rights & Advocacy Groups

Organizations like the ACLU, NAACP, and local civil liberties groups rely on volunteers to assist with research, community outreach, and event coordination.

Where to find: Visit websites of national organizations for local chapter information. Many have specific student volunteer programs.
🌎

Immigrant & Refugee Services

Help immigrants navigate legal processes by volunteering with refugee resettlement agencies or immigration legal clinics. Translation skills are a plus.

Where to find: Look for local USCIS-affiliated organizations, International Rescue Committee, Catholic Charities immigration services, or university immigration clinics.
📚

Tutoring & Mentoring Youth

Teach or mentor younger students, particularly in under-served communities. This demonstrates commitment to education and equity—values admissions committees respect.

Where to find: Big Brothers Big Sisters, Boys & Girls Clubs, school district volunteer programs, or local literacy organizations.
🛠

Habitat for Humanity & Housing Orgs

Housing law is a major area of legal practice. Volunteering in affordable housing shows awareness of systemic issues and community dedication.

Where to find: Habitat for Humanity local chapters, local housing authorities, or tenant advocacy organizations in your area.
📄

Political Campaigns & Civic Engagement

Volunteer on local political campaigns, voter registration drives, or ballot initiative efforts. This shows engagement with the democratic process and policy.

Where to find: Local political party offices, League of Women Voters, voter registration organizations, or contact your elected officials' offices directly.
🤝

Mediation & Conflict Resolution

Some community mediation centers train volunteers to help resolve disputes. This experience is directly relevant to alternative dispute resolution (ADR) in law.

Where to find: Community mediation centers, local bar association programs, or school-based peer mediation programs.

LSAT Preparation

The LSAT is one of the most important factors in law school admissions. It tests logical reasoning, analytical reasoning, and reading comprehension—skills you can build over time.

Logical Reasoning

Analyze arguments, identify flaws, draw conclusions, and strengthen or weaken claims. This is the largest section of the test.

~50% of scored questions

Analytical Reasoning

Often called "logic games"—involves deducing relationships from sets of rules. Practice with formal logic and puzzles.

~25% of scored questions

Reading Comprehension

Read dense passages from law, humanities, sciences, and social sciences, then answer questions about structure, arguments, and implications.

~25% of scored questions
1

Start Early

Begin LSAT preparation at least 3–6 months before your test date. Many top scorers study for even longer. Use official LSAT PrepTests from LSAC for the most realistic practice.

2

Take a Course or Self-Study

Consider LSAT prep courses from providers like Khan Academy (free), 7Sage, Blueprint, or Kaplan. Self-disciplined students can succeed with books and practice tests alone.

3

Score Targets

The LSAT is scored 120–180. Top-14 law schools generally look for 170+. Scores of 160+ open doors to many strong programs. Know your target schools' medians.

4

Practice Under Real Conditions

Take full-length, timed practice tests to build stamina. Review every wrong answer thoroughly. Track your progress and focus on weak areas.

Skills to Develop Now

Beyond academics, successful law students share a common set of skills. Start building these abilities in high school and college.

✍

Writing

Clear, persuasive, and precise writing is the foundation of legal practice. Write often—essays, editorials, research papers, and journals.

💬

Public Speaking

Whether in a courtroom, boardroom, or negotiation, lawyers must communicate confidently and clearly to diverse audiences.

🧠

Critical Thinking

Lawyers must analyze problems from all angles, evaluate evidence, and construct sound logical arguments every day.

🔎

Research

Legal research is a core attorney skill. Practice finding, evaluating, and synthesizing information from multiple sources.

👥

Empathy & Listening

Great lawyers listen to their clients and understand diverse perspectives. Empathy builds trust and leads to better advocacy.

⏱

Time Management

Law school and legal practice require juggling deadlines, cases, and clients. Build strong organizational habits early.

Law School Application Tips

When it's time to apply to law school, your preparation will pay off. Here are key strategies for building a strong application.

1

Maintain a High GPA

Your undergraduate GPA is one of the two most important numbers in your application (alongside LSAT). Aim for the highest GPA you can achieve. Law schools look at your LSAC-calculated GPA, which includes all undergraduate coursework.

2

Write a Compelling Personal Statement

Your personal statement should tell a genuine story about why you want to practice law. Focus on specific experiences that shaped your desire, not generic statements about "wanting justice." Show, don't tell.

3

Get Strong Letters of Recommendation

Build relationships with professors who know you well. The best recommendations come from faculty who can speak to your writing, analytical thinking, and character—not just confirm you got an A.

4

Demonstrate Sustained Commitment

Admissions committees prefer deep involvement in a few activities over shallow participation in many. Show years of commitment and increasing responsibility in your extracurriculars and volunteer work.

5

Research Schools Carefully

Look beyond rankings. Consider each school's clinical programs, faculty specialties, bar passage rates, employment outcomes, location, and culture. Apply to a range of reach, target, and safety schools.

6

Apply Early

Most law schools use rolling admissions. Submitting your application early in the cycle (September–November) can improve your chances, as more seats are available earlier in the process.
